1
Poor soul, the center of my sinful earth,
Pressed with these rebel powers that thee array,
Why dost thou pine within and suffer dearth,?

"This thy heart's purpose is to love me," said I. "Thou art mine; what more should it be than this one?"

I replied: Thou hast no other treasure but thine! The world itself alone hath made you rich by its own beauty--and yet there remaineth a portion for all eternity wherest we are born.'


2
My love is as a fever, longing still
For that which longer nurseth the disease,
Feeding on that which doth preserve the ill, or of any other.
O sweet thing I have seen my joy grow; but yet what now hath it been?
To make myself happy by thy beauty's light!
When your sweetness grows old and dies in you all this time — how can thou be so good to me again?


3

4
Canst thou, O cruel, say I love thee not
When I against myself with thee partake?
Do I not think on thee when I forgot me in thy name; nor do my heart's sweet song sing: for it is so great a mark of truth!
And yet if the praise be to you that art written (which no longer will live) what should this mean then—that our hearts are such as ours alone.

5
O, from what power hast thou this powerful might
With insufficiency my heart to sway?
To make me give the lie to my true sight,
In order that I may be more secure in thy name.


6
Love is too young to know what conscience is;
Yet who knows not conscience is born of love?
Then, gentle cheater, urge not my amiss,

But in the midst of thy weakness thou hast made me full.


7
In loving thee thou know'st I am forsworn,
But thou art twice forsworn, to me love swearing;
In act thy bed-vow broke, and new faith torn.


8
Cupid laid by his brand and fell asleep.
A maid of Dian's this advantage found,
And his love-kindling fire did quickly steep;
He that he lay in sleep made a sudden flower:* *For it was so she looked to him with her eyes open,
That at night the flame on which you had left forth your eye should burn.*
When thou art fairly done thy beauty shines out again! This is my darling sweetheart who has lost thee--
O dear poor man not wherewithst I make life from me must be lived."

10
When my love swears that she is made of truth,
I do believe her, though I know she lies,
That she might think me some untutored youth,
And may call herself a piece of the morning's trifling;.
But it must be said with all humility what thy child shall say:—a stranger hath no right to speak this word!
Thou art such an honest and fair man as thou dost lie awake in woe?
